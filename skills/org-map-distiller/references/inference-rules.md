# Inference Rules

Use these rules before turning records into people, relationship, or graph claims.

## Inference Order

1. Extract events.
2. Bind evidence to events.
3. Extract candidate relationship signals from events.
4. Assign relationship type, direction, and confidence.
5. Aggregate repeated behavior into person profiles.
6. Synthesize network patterns.
7. Convert missing or weak evidence into open questions.

## Person Inference Rules

- Describe observed behavior before interpreting personality.
- Separate formal role, observed role, informal role, network position, behavior pattern, reputation, and inferred motive.
- Use "in the available events" to bound claims to the dataset.
- Require multiple events before describing a repeated pattern.
- Require alternative explanations for negative judgments.
- Treat reputation claims as claims by a source, not as facts.
- Treat motive as low confidence unless the person states it directly or repeated evidence strongly supports it.
- Avoid MBTI-style reduction and private psychological diagnosis.

## Relationship Inference Rules

- Every relationship must have direction, type, evidence events, confidence, and at least one alternative explanation when the claim is sensitive.
- Do not write "A and B are close" without specifying trust, dependency, alliance, information flow, emotional support, or another observable type.
- Allow multiple relationship types between the same people.
- Distinguish open conflict from avoidance, task conflict from status conflict, and disagreement from hostility.
- Distinguish formal authority from informal influence.
- Distinguish delegation from responsibility shifting by looking for clarity, authority, support, and accountability.
- Distinguish information sharing from gatekeeping by looking for withholding, selective routing, and chokepoints.

## Power and Responsibility Rules

Power may come from title, resource control, expertise, decision rights, information access, reputation, relationship with authority, or ability to block work.

Responsibility movement should identify:

- who owned the issue before
- who owned it after
- whether ownership became clearer or more ambiguous
- whether authority moved with responsibility
- whether blame or cleanup moved after failure

Do not claim "wants power but not responsibility" unless repeated events show decision influence, accountability avoidance, and responsibility transfer.

## Text-Only Evidence Limits

If only LINE, email, or meeting notes are available, you can infer visible interaction and documented patterns. You usually cannot infer:

- private motives
- stable personality
- hidden alliances
- offline intimidation
- true loyalty or disloyalty
- competence outside the sampled work
- full organizational power

Mark these as open questions or low-confidence hypotheses.

## Contradiction Rules

Contradiction is data. Record it when:

- two sources describe the same event differently
- behavior differs across channels
- formal role conflicts with observed role
- one event supports trust and another supports conflict
- a person appears central in one workflow and peripheral in another

Do not average contradictions away. Preserve them and state what additional evidence would resolve them.

## Ethical Rules

- Minimize private personal detail that is irrelevant to the work relationship.
- Prefer aliases when output may circulate.
- Avoid defamatory certainty.
- Warn when a claim could be weaponized against someone.
- State that outputs are analytical artifacts based on available records, not final truth about people.
