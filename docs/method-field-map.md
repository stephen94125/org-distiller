# Method Field Map

Use this file when grounding an organization relationship map in a wider research frame. The goal is not to cite every theory in the output, but to choose the right lens for the data and avoid reducing group life to casual personality judgment.

## Field Map

This skill sits at the intersection of:

- Small group research: how roles, norms, cohesion, conflict, and leadership emerge in small human groups.
- Sociometry: mapping choices, preferences, attractions, rejections, subgroups, and social positions.
- Role theory: separating formal titles, expected roles, enacted roles, role conflict, and role strain.
- Symbolic interactionism: interpreting meaning, identity, face, and role negotiation through interaction.
- Dramaturgical analysis: reading frontstage/backstage behavior, impression management, face-saving, and audience effects.
- Social exchange theory: modeling reciprocity, obligations, dependence, costs, benefits, and perceived fairness.
- Group dynamics: studying cohesion, influence, norms, conflict, conformity, and subgroup formation.
- Informal organization: identifying unofficial authority, advice networks, social coalitions, and hidden decision routes.
- Bureaucracy and organization theory: distinguishing formal authority, procedures, hierarchy, coordination, and dysfunction.
- Power, status, hierarchy, and authority: separating command authority, expertise, control of resources, reputation, and agenda control.
- Social capital, weak ties, structural holes, and brokerage: identifying bridges, brokers, boundary spanners, and access to nonredundant information.
- Trust, conflict, coalition, clique, and faction: detecting cooperative and adversarial structures without overclaiming motive.
- Sensemaking, organizational culture, and communities of practice: interpreting shared meanings, routines, local language, and tacit expertise.
- Organizational behavior and management: applying the above to teams, departments, family businesses, decision networks, psychological safety, team topology, responsibility gaps, and knowledge management.
- Personality, role, and interaction-style inference: using personality psychology only where useful, including Big Five, interpersonal circumplex, attachment patterns, political skill, emotional labor, and the difference between role behavior and stable personality.
- Social Network Analysis and Organizational Network Analysis: quantifying centrality, brokerage, structural holes, density, reciprocity, clustering, formal vs informal network differences, and survey-based, digital-trace-based, or mixed-method ONA.
- Qualitative research: using ethnography, participant observation, interview, contextual inquiry, grounded theory, discourse analysis, conversation analysis, critical incident technique, process tracing, and interaction episodes.
- Knowledge graph and AI methods: entity-relation extraction, event extraction, knowledge graph construction, social graph construction, hypergraph personality or higher-order relations, GraphRAG, LLM-assisted coding, summarization, relationship extraction, and contradiction detection.

## What Each Lens Contributes

| Lens | Use it to answer | Do not use it to |
|---|---|---|
| Small group research | What recurring roles, norms, and conflicts appear? | Treat a group as a static org chart |
| Sociometry | Who chooses, avoids, consults, or rejects whom? | Infer private feelings from weak traces |
| Role theory | What is formal role vs enacted role? | Collapse role behavior into personality |
| Symbolic interactionism | How do people negotiate meaning and identity? | Mind-read motives without text evidence |
| Dramaturgical analysis | Who performs authority, deference, face-saving, or blame? | Overinterpret every message as strategy |
| Social exchange theory | Who gives, receives, owes, depends, or reciprocates? | Reduce all interaction to calculation |
| Informal organization | Who has real influence outside the org chart? | Claim hidden power without repeated evidence |
| SNA/ONA | Who is central, bridging, isolated, reciprocal, or clustered? | Treat metrics as truth without context |
| Personality and interaction style | What repeated behavioral or communication pattern is visible? | Reduce the person to MBTI, diagnosis, or one stable essence |
| Qualitative methods | What do incidents mean in local context? | Ignore patterns because they are not numeric |
| Knowledge graph/LLM | Extract entities, events, relations, contradictions, and retrieval paths | Hallucinate relationships or false precision |

## Method Selection

Use text records such as LINE, email, meeting notes, and documents for:

- event extraction
- interaction sequence reconstruction
- decision and responsibility movement
- visible information flow
- repeated communication patterns
- explicit conflict, trust, requests, delegation, escalation, avoidance, and bypass

Use interviews and observation for:

- hidden power
- fear, trust, avoidance, and informal norms
- backstage interpretations
- reputation and role expectations
- missing offline context
- disconfirming hypotheses from text traces

Use quantitative network methods for:

- centrality
- brokerage
- structural holes
- density
- reciprocity
- clustering
- advice, trust, conflict, communication, and decision networks
- formal org chart vs informal network comparison
- survey-based ONA
- digital trace-based ONA
- mixed-method ONA

Use qualitative interpretation for:

- power and status
- conflict type
- trust and psychological safety
- emotional labor and emotional burden
- responsibility shifting and ambiguity absorption
- sensemaking, culture, and communities of practice

Use AI and knowledge graph methods for:

- entity and alias resolution
- event extraction
- relation extraction
- evidence binding
- contradiction detection
- graph summaries
- GraphRAG over event and relationship records
- combining network metrics with qualitative memos

Use personality and interaction-style concepts carefully for:

- Big Five style behavioral hypotheses when repeated evidence exists
- interpersonal circumplex patterns such as dominance, warmth, hostility, or submission as interaction signals
- attachment-like patterns only as cautious relational hypotheses, not clinical labels
- political skill as observable influence, networking, social astuteness, and apparent sincerity in organizational settings
- emotional labor as repeated affect management, reassurance, mediation, or pressure absorption
- role behavior vs stable personality separation
- limits of inferring personality from language, behavior, network position, and repeated incidents

## Company and Small-Group Application Questions

When applying the skill in a company, department, family business, lab, community, or small team, ask:

- What does the formal org chart say?
- What does the informal org chart show?
- Who does the boss actually listen to?
- Who controls information?
- Who is the bottleneck?
- Who repairs exceptions or fills workflow holes?
- Who bypasses whom?
- Who appears to receive authority without responsibility, or responsibility without authority?
- Who is an emotional center, conflict source, mediator, or pressure absorber?
- Which judgments require more evidence before they can be used?

Modern ONA plus survey plus LLM products, such as Teamspective, can be useful examples of contemporary company application. Do not make SaaS products the center of the analysis; use them only to understand current implementation patterns and limitations.

## What Can and Cannot Be Known From Records

From LINE, email, and meeting notes, infer visible interaction patterns, explicit decisions, requests, response patterns, escalation, responsibility movement, and documented conflict. Do not infer private motives, stable personality, offline alliances, hidden emotions, or real loyalty without corroborating evidence.

From interviews and observation, infer context and local meaning, but treat testimony as one data source with perspective and incentives. Bind interview claims to source, speaker, and confidence.

From network metrics, infer structural position, not moral character or competence. A central person may be influential, overloaded, blocking, coordinating, or simply copied on many messages.

## Canonical Reading Directions

Use stable classics and research directions when the user asks for background or a research report. Do not invent page numbers, DOIs, or sources. If uncertain, mark uncertainty.

- Small group research and group dynamics: Kurt Lewin, Bales interaction process analysis, Homans, Cartwright and Zander, and later group dynamics handbooks.
- Sociometry and social networks: Jacob Moreno, sociograms, Granovetter on weak ties, Burt on structural holes, social capital research, Wasserman and Faust, Borgatti/Everett/Johnson.
- Role, interaction, and dramaturgy: Mead, Blumer, Goffman, role theory, symbolic interactionism, impression management, face-work.
- Exchange, power, and organization: Blau, Emerson, Weber on bureaucracy, March and Simon, Cyert and March, Crozier, Mintzberg, Pfeffer, and organization theory handbooks.
- Culture, sensemaking, and practice: Weick on sensemaking, Schein on organizational culture, Lave and Wenger on communities of practice.
- Trust and conflict: Deutsch, Lewicki, Mayer/Davis/Schoorman, Jehn, psychological safety research associated with Edmondson.
- Qualitative methods: Glaser and Strauss, Strauss and Corbin, Charmaz, Yin, Geertz, Emerson/Fretz/Shaw, conversation analysis and discourse analysis traditions.
- Personality and interaction: Big Five research, interpersonal circumplex, attachment research, political skill, and emotional labor research associated with Hochschild.
- AI and graph methods: entity-relation extraction, event extraction, knowledge graphs, social graph extraction, hypergraphs, GraphRAG, LLM-assisted qualitative coding, and hallucination mitigation.

## Skill Design Principles From The Research Frame

1. Model events before relationships and people.
2. Keep person image separate from interpersonal network.
3. Keep role behavior separate from stable personality.
4. Bind every relationship to event evidence.
5. Distinguish formal role, observed role, network position, reputation, and inferred motive.
6. Combine qualitative interpretation with optional network metrics.
7. Use open questions when evidence is missing.
8. Treat contradiction as signal, not noise.
9. Avoid hallucinated relationships through source pointers and confidence labels.
10. Preserve ethical guardrails around privacy, defamation, power asymmetry, and weaponization.
